How we position JSS with DP

JSS is DP's specialized dealer — the only dealer DP earns a profit share from. We run front-line device sales like any dealer, but use our own application to own the long-term renewal business.
All we ask of DP: database access and background support. Everything else — the app, the channel, the operations — we own.

What "specialized dealer" actually means

It is not a software-vendor pitch. We enter DP's world in a language DP already understands — as a dealer — but play two roles at once.

Role 1 — Dealer

The front face

We issue and sell new devices on the ground, exactly like DP's existing dealers. Fresh issuance is the visible, everyday activity.

Role 2 — Distributor

The network builder

We onboard sub-dealers in areas DP can't directly reach, run them on our application, and route their renewals back through us.

The difference from every other dealer in one line: DP has a stake in this dealership and shares the profit. For all other dealers, DP earns nothing on the margin — so DP will naturally prioritise the one it profits from.

Stakeholder relationships

DP profits only from the JSS branch. That single green loop is why DP backs us over any other dealer.

The exchange

What we need from DP
  • Access to the deployed device database
  • Background support & prioritisation
  • Nothing that costs DP margin or cash
What DP gets in return
  • A direct profit share on this dealership
  • Seamless operations: payment consolidation, dealer management in one portal
  • A platform that runs renewals long after the government contract ends

Why the model holds — and why the app is the point

1. The app is not for the end user — it's for owning renewals.

Most of the base is individual cab owners who buy mainly for the certificate; the app is a genuine need only for aggregators. So end-user demand isn't the reason to build it. The app's real job is operational control and capturing the recurring renewal revenue.

How the money actually works

Dealers are the channel for acquisition. The app is the engine for retention — and retention is where the durable money is.

2. Whoever owns the app owns the renewals.

This isn't theory — the market already runs this way. The players who control the software keep the recurring revenue; the ones who don't, lose it. Positioning as DP's app-powered specialized dealer puts us on the winning side of that split.

3. The long game: after the government contract, the app runs everything.

Once the current contract cycle ends, operations, auto-renewals and dealer management all move onto the platform. The app stops being a feature and becomes the thing the whole business depends on — which is precisely why DP should want to be inside the dealership that owns it.
